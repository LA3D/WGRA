The document graph, live

Stage 2 · Ingestion · real output from the extraction pipeline

The extraction pipeline turns a source document into a typed RDF graph: the document at the center, section chunks around it, and the concepts each chunk mentions fanning out. Every concept carries two @type slots — one canonical (SPARQL-queryable across the whole corpus) and one domain-specific (grown from the source, no pre-declaration). The graph on the left is a real slice from a real briefing; the JSON on the right is the actual output.

   "schema:CreativeWork",

Two @type slots: canonical → SPARQL, ex: → corpus-grown.

This is a real graph from the extraction pipeline: a briefing document about claims-based extraction and retrieval, run through the pipeline, output as JSON-LD. Fifteen chunks; five shown here. Each chunk carries its extracted concepts as skos:member edges.

The point to make: every concept has a two-slot @type. The first slot is canonical (skos:Concept) so it’s SPARQL-queryable across the corpus. The second slot is domain-specific (ex:design_principle, ex:trust_tier, etc.) — the ontology accretes from the source without pre-declaration. Fixed ontologies force the corpus into a schema chosen in advance; this keeps the closed-envelope queryability and the open-payload growth in the same node.

Drag a Concept node to inspect it. The interactive graph is doing the same work as three static slides would have.

Dense RAG breaks on the bridges.

The retrieval problem

Dense RAG: embed the question, cosine against chunk embeddings, take top-K, hand to the LLM. One-shot similarity.

  • Works for hop 1 — the passage that lexically matches the question.
  • Breaks on multi-hop — bridge and answer chunks share no vocabulary with the question.
  • Breaks at scale — with a real corpus of distractors, similar-but-wrong passages drown the true evidence.

Multi-hop retrieval is a graph traversal problem in disguise.

Dense retrieval is a hop-1 tool. Multi-hop needs structure, not more similarity.

The failure is measured, not asserted. On the full 21k-chunk corpus, the first (directly-matched) gold chunk stays at rank 1 at any corpus size — cosine finds what it can find. The deepest 4-hop bridge chunk sinks to median rank ~1,800. all-gold@30 collapses from 88% (small clean set) to 22% (full corpus). Dense RAG solves hop 1 and collapses on the bridges. The rest of this section shows what structure buys you.

The missing layer: durable synthesis

Transition · from retrieval to memory

Extraction gives you records. Analysis gives you answers. Neither gives you memory — knowledge that compounds across sessions and researchers.

Today that understanding lives in someone’s head, in stale notebooks, in README fragments. It walks out when the person leaves.

What a synthesis layer needs

  • Human + agent readable · one substrate, no lock-in

  • Queryable as a graph · typed edges → SPARQL KG

This is the transition slide from the retrieval work to the wiki work. Today it lives in whoever’s head is closest to the work. The rest of the talk is about the pattern that fills that gap.

The template’s two ingestion modes

Direct-ingest · schema-extraction into wiki pages

For a group’s own papers, a thesis lit review, a research-execution wiki.

Each source becomes a typed page (SourceSummary, LiteratureNote, MethodNote) with frontmatter edges (extends, supports, criticizes, up).

The KG is over pages — categorically different from the per-document parse graph on slides 2-3.

markov_embeddings_and_rag, AI-Sci-Disc-Mem, SavoieAgentKnowledge, web_forager — all direct-ingest.

Synthesis over a pipeline · above the document-parse graph

For large corpora where you first run extraction + retrieval — slides 2-5’s paper-explorer, document graph, 21,000-chunk MuSiQue.

The pipeline document-parses; the wiki sits above it, capturing what survived cross-checking and what was walked back.

earth616 — synthesis on top of the extraction pipeline.

Same substrate, same discipline gates, same SPARQL queries — categorically different graph. The rest of the talk shows the direct-ingest mode.

Important framing point: llm-wiki-memory-template is not just a synthesis layer that sits above a large extraction pipeline. It’s the memory substrate, and it has two natural entry points.

The two modes produce categorically different graphs, not scaled versions of the same thing. Priscila is developing this distinction as an explicit taxonomy on the crcresearch/data-extraction-wiki-memory wiki (see the Schema-Extraction-vs-Document-Parsing page): schema-driven extraction fills predefined structure (like the SourceSummary/MethodNote/LiteratureNote page types in a wiki), while document parsing reconstructs the whole page (layout, reading order, tables, figures — what the paper-explorer on slide 2 shows). Different tasks, different training, different outputs. Confusing the two is the most common source of wasted implementation effort in extraction work.

Direct-ingest is what most academic work actually needs. You have your group’s own papers (or your thesis’s cited literature, or your course’s reading list) — dozens to hundreds of sources, not millions of chunks. Each paper deserves its own wiki page with frontmatter that says what it argues, what it extends, what it criticizes. The KG that builds is a semantic graph OVER pages, not per-document extractions. That’s what AI-Sci-Disc-Mem does (115 papers × 20 topics). That’s what SavoieAgentKnowledge does (91 papers × 7 research problems). That’s what the web_forager case study you’ll see next uses.

Synthesis-over-a-pipeline is what you do at extraction scale. When you’re running MuSiQue at 21,000 chunks, the wiki isn’t ingesting the raw chunks — it’s synthesizing what the pipeline learned. Which experiments survived, which drifted, which were walked back. That’s what markov_embeddings_and_rag does.

Same template. Same discipline gates. Same SPARQL substrate. Different starting point. The rest of the talk mostly shows direct-ingest, because that’s what will resonate with most of the audience — but the same wiki works for both.

Failure paths, preserved not deleted.

Memory that compounds · the file-drawer effect, Rosenthal 1979

Journals publish the win.

The abandoned method, the walked-back claim, the metric bug that inflated an earlier result — all deleted from the record.

Every group has a student who repeated a failed method because no one told them.

The wiki keeps the trajectory.

When an approach turns out wrong, the corrected successor is filed alongside the original with a cross-reference back; the failure stays in place with a Status note pointing forward.

A verification gate ensures new claims land alongside the ones they qualify, not on top of them.

A reader arriving a year later encounters the trajectory, not just the answer.

The file-drawer effect (Rosenthal 1979) is the canonical failure mode of scientific record-keeping: only positive results get written up, the abandoned methods and walked-back claims never enter the literature. The wiki-grounded pattern is append-only by convention (Saboia Moreira et al., 2026). Corrected pages are filed alongside originals, not on top of them. A verification gate enforces this at commit time. The reader gets the trajectory, not just the answer — which is what makes the pattern durable across student generations.

The bug cascaded across Experiments 7, 8, and 11. The graph, not a debugger, told the reviewer where the fix had to reach.

The LLM refusing to hallucinate looked identical to the LLM answering. The search stopped in exactly the case where deeper searching was warranted.

The specific mechanism to name: the frontmatter extends: edge on Experiment 11 pointed to Experiment 7. Experiment 7 pointed to Experiment 8. Following those typed edges backward surfaced the fact that the same inherited analytic frame was in all three experiments — so the bug was too.

The bug itself: the pipeline had a check that treated the model’s hedge (“not visible on this page”) as a positive extraction. Every time the LLM was being honest about not seeing an entity, the pipeline was recording that as a successful answer AND stopping the search — meaning it never went to the next page where the entity might actually be visible.

Real coverage on the flagship customer was 8 of 17, not 17 of 17. And the fix had to cascade: not just Experiment 11 (where the finding surfaced), but back through 7 and 8 too. The typed-edge chain told the reviewer where the cascade needed to reach.

What made this possible

Case study · what worked

Two authors, two sessions, no coordination.

Both in Claude Code, no handoff meeting — the wiki carried the state.

The typed extends: edge is what made the cascade legible.

Body links would have said “these are related somehow.” The typed edge said “this experiment inherits its analytic frame from that one; if that is wrong, this one probably is too.”

75% coverage recovery after the fix cascaded back through two prior experiments.

Extends chain plus the two criticism edges. Same shape as the demo-branch cascade the reviewer walked.

Full case study: la3d.github.io/WGRA/wiki-collaboration-story/

This does not happen with RAG. Similarity search cannot walk semantic edges backward across experiments.

Three claims worth landing before moving on:

  1. Two-author collaboration mechanism worked. No coordination overhead — each author was in their own Claude Code session on their own branch, and the wiki was the connective tissue. That is what “team scale” looks like in practice.

  2. The typed edge was load-bearing. A body hyperlink would have said “there’s a connection somewhere.” The typed extends: edge said “this page inherits its analytic frame from that one; if the parent is wrong, this one probably is too.” That semantic content is what let the reviewer’s agent walk the cascade.

  3. 75% coverage recovery is a real number on the flagship entity. The fix cascaded backward through 2 prior experiments; each got a commit and a page update. The wiki preserved the trajectory — original error still findable, correction filed alongside — exactly as failure-path preservation is designed to work.

Contrast with RAG: similarity search can retrieve similar chunks. It cannot walk semantic edges backward. That’s the argument for typed structure over embedding-only retrieval.

Team scale · collaborative within a group

The wiki carries the trail across collaborators

Lab notebooks. Slack. Google Docs. Jupyter notebooks. Half-remembered meeting slides. Protocol binders. Thesis drafts.

A postdoc and a grad student on the same project keep notes across all of these in parallel silos. When the PI checks in a month later, most of the trail is scattered or lost.

One attributed wiki instead.

Every log entry carries a by: <human> via <agent> line. Git preserves the amendment history. Concurrent writes: mechanical conflicts resolve automatically; semantic ones route through an LLM-assisted merge.

The Web Forager case study demonstrated this in practice: two authors, two Claude Code sessions, no synchronous coordination. The wiki was the connective tissue between the two branches. Every log entry had by: <human> via <agent> attribution, so the reviewer could trace who claimed what without asking the person.

Mechanically: the wiki-write-protocol handles concurrent writes through git’s optimistic-push with retry, plus .gitattributes merge=union for append-only files. When there’s a semantic conflict (two people editing the same page’s frontmatter), an llm_resolve skill routes the merge through the LLM. Both edits land with correct attribution.

Agent capability · built-in workflow

Discipline in the artifact, not in the head of whoever prompts best

Raw group subscription: every student prompts differently. The discipline lives in memory. It walks out with them.

Configured environment: the discipline is in the repo. Every session starts from a known baseline. Any agent that reads .claude/skills/ gets it.

  • Four operations as single commands: wiki-source, wiki-experiment, wiki-lint, plus the verification gate at commit time.
  • SessionStart hook injects the wiki index + last 5 log entries at every session boot. Agent starts oriented, not blank.
  • Discipline gates catch common rationalizations before commit — e.g. “I’ll add the corpus tag later” becomes a warning right when it appears.
---

description: File experiment results to the wiki
  following the SCHEMA Ingest procedure. Use after
  a run produces metrics, ablations, or comparisons
  worth keeping as durable project memory.
---

# Procedure
1. Read existing pages before writing.
2. Create or update the experiment-results page:
   type: synthesis or entity,
   up: pointing to the benchmark page,
   typed edges (supports:, criticizes:) when
   the result confirms or refutes prior claims.
3. Body: config, headline metrics, what changed,
   what was surprising, link to results/ dir.
4. Verification gate: every numerical claim traces
   to real script output. No projections.

The wiki-experiment skill file from the template. Every project instantiated from crcresearch/llm-wiki-memory-template gets the same procedure, model-agnostic.

Say: “The workflow isn’t a Slack channel where senior people remind juniors of best practices. It ships as skill files in the repo. Every session, the SessionStart hook injects the wiki index and the last five log entries, so the agent starts oriented instead of starting blank. The verification gate then fires at commit time — you can’t file a claim like ‘73.3% on MuSiQue’ unless the wiki page traces to real script output. Projection-as-fact detection at the artifact level, not at the reviewer’s discretion.”

The four operations that ship as single commands: wiki-source (ingest a source PDF or docset), wiki-experiment (file experiment results), wiki-lint (health-check the wiki), and the verification gate (fires as a PostToolUse advisory hook). All are in the base template.

The multi-AI story is really about durability. The AI vendor landscape churns every six months — a group Claude subscription might be a Cursor + Copilot subscription next semester and something else after that. If your workflow is locked to a specific vendor, you migrate every six months. If your workflow is a wiki plus a set of skill files, adopting a new agent is writing an overlay, not a migration. Same wiki, same policy files, same discipline gates. The via: <agent> field on every log entry is what makes it auditable which tool did which write.

Federation is the multi-wiki story. Each group is autonomous — its own wiki, its own permissions, its own git remote. The interoperation layer is an “agent card” (published by each wiki) that describes what the group knows about and how to reach it.

Your agent can then issue a query to a peer group’s wiki. Practical example: your colleague in comp-chem answered a DFT-functional-benchmarks question last year and filed it in their wiki. You don’t have to email them — your agent asks their agent, gets a cited response back with links to specific pages in their wiki, and files the answer in yours with the attribution preserved.

The ask mechanism (clone-and-invoke claude -p in the peer wiki) is in template v0.1.0. The message/post mailbox skill is not yet published; it’s a maintainer-machine-only artifact today.

One ontology, every wiki: the semantic foundation

Shared substrate · llm-wiki-colab

28 classes, four groups.

  • Core concept · Concept, Entity, Tool
  • Note types · Literature, Method, Theory, Implementation, …
  • Structure pages · Index, MOC, Decision, Finding, …
  • Person / Org

36 semantic properties connect them: extends, supports, criticizes, related, up, authorOf, defines, source, …

The ontology ships at la3d.github.io/llm-wiki-colab. Every wiki instantiated from the template inherits it — no per-project schema work.

SHACL shapes validate structure at commit time. Eleven canned SPARQL queries (hub-notes, ancestors-of, supports-criticizes, …) ship in scripts/kg/sparql/.

Next slide: this ontology applied to a real research wiki — 51 pages, 1,162 triples, live.

Wiki-page frontmatter uses typed edges — extends:, supports:, criticizes:, related:, up:. The KG-build pipeline (scripts/kg/build-graph.sh) walks the wiki, reads each page’s frontmatter, and emits an RDF triple per typed edge. The result is a real semantic graph — 28-class ontology from the shared llm-wiki-colab project, SHACL-validated, queryable with SPARQL.

The template ships eleven canned SPARQL queries: hub-notes (this slide), ancestors-of (transitive up traversal), supports-criticizes (evidence edges), orphan-notes (unlinked pages), and others. Each is executed in-process via rdflib against the built graph-full.ttl. Deep dive: la3d.github.io/WGRA/knowledge-graph/.
